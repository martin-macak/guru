Selection-Centered Subgraph

The graph stays bounded around the focused node. Movement is semantic, not spatial: changing focus reshapes the local neighborhood.

Pros

  • Terminal-friendly and readable
  • Good fit for keyboard traversal
  • Keeps investigation flow centered on one artifact

Cons

  • Not a true canvas
  • Large topologies are seen incrementally

Free-Panning Graph Canvas

A larger graph lives on a spatial canvas. The user pans and zooms to inspect regions, more like a map than a semantic browser.

Pros

  • Shows broad structure at once
  • Feels closest to a graph explorer

Cons

  • Harder to read in terminal space
  • Keyboard model is heavier
  • Easy to drift away from the active investigation

List-First Graph

The graph is primarily a structured neighbor list or edge table. A small preview exists, but traversal is mostly table-driven.

Pros

  • Simple to implement and test
  • Very stable in terminal layouts

Cons

  • Feels less distinctive
  • Loses graph shape and path intuition

Recommendation

For your stated goals, A. Selection-Centered Subgraph is the strongest default. It preserves a real graph pane, keeps keyboard traversal crisp, and avoids the terminal UX cost of a freeform canvas.
